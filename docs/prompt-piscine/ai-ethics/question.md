# AI Ethics — Scenario-Based Re-Coding Questions
> Derived strictly from Exercise 00: Learning to Use AI Ethically as a Coder  
> Format: Scenario-based | Intensity: 10+ questions  
> Rules: Answer each question in your own words. No AI-generated responses.

---

## Question 1

You land your first dev job. Your team is under pressure to ship a feature fast. A senior engineer hands you a block of AI-generated code, tells you to test it and push it. You run it — it works on the happy path. You don't fully understand how it handles edge cases.

**What do you do, and why? What does this moment reveal about the gap between "it works" and "I understand it"?**

---

## Question 2

It's 2 AM. A critical bug has hit production. Your team lead is unreachable. The codebase you're looking at was mostly written with AI assistance — you reviewed it but never deeply understood the logic. You have 30 minutes before clients start losing data.

**Walk through what happens next. What does this scenario expose about the real cost of skipping understanding when using AI tools?**

---

## Question 3

You're in a technical interview. The interviewer asks: "How would you design a caching system for a high-traffic API?" You've built things before — mostly by prompting AI and adapting its output. You never had to explain the reasoning from scratch.

**What happens in that room? Using the exercise's Learner A vs Learner B framework, which learner walks out with the job offer — and why?**

## Question 4

A classmate shows you their palindrome function. It's clean, well-commented, and handles edge cases perfectly. You ask how they built it — they say they just asked ChatGPT, copied it, and moved on. It passed all tests.

**According to the exercise's principles, what did your classmate actually fail to do — even though the code is correct? What did they lose that can't be recovered by reading the code after the fact?**

## Question 5

You're working on a team project. Your personal AI ethics contract (as described in Part D of the exercise) says: *"I will not use AI when I haven't tried the problem myself."* But your teammate keeps pasting AI-generated solutions into the shared repo without attempting anything first. The code works. Your deadline is tomorrow.

**Do you say something? What's the tension here between team productivity and the principles of the fairness contract? How do you navigate it?**

## Question 6

You're assigned to extend a palindrome checker. The requirements now say: ignore spaces and punctuation, make it case-insensitive, and return the exact position where the string stops being a palindrome.

You wrote the original function yourself. Your teammate, who copied theirs from AI, is also asked to make the same changes.

**Who completes the task faster and with more confidence — and why? Use the exercise's reasoning about mental models and struggle to explain the difference.**

## Question 7

You rate yourself on the five skills from Part F of the exercise:
- Problem decomposition
- Systems thinking
- Critical evaluation
- Debugging mindset
- Conceptual understanding (the "why")

You score yourself a 2 out of 5 on **debugging mindset**. The exercise asks for 3 specific actions this week to improve it — without outsourcing thinking to AI.

**What are your 3 concrete actions? They must be specific, realistic, and cannot involve asking AI to debug for you.**

## Question 8

A new developer joins your team. They've been using AI to code since they started learning. They're fast — they ship features quickly. But when you pair-program with them, you notice they can't explain why their code works, only that it does.

**Using the exercise's framework, write the honest feedback you'd give them. What risk are they carrying into their career that they may not see yet?**

## Question 9

You've been following your fairness contract for two weeks. You attempt problems for at least 20 minutes before asking AI. Today, you're stuck on a problem for 45 minutes and feel completely lost. You haven't made meaningful progress.

**At what point, if any, does asking AI become the right call? How do you ask AI in a way that still makes you the learner — not just the copy-paster? Write out the exact prompt you'd use.**

## Question 10

A company releases a new AI coding tool that can generate full production-ready modules from a one-line description. Your manager says: "We're adopting this. It'll replace most of the junior coding work."

**Using the skills and mindset described in Part F and Part E of the exercise, argue: what makes a developer irreplaceable even in this environment? Be specific — reference the skills the exercise identifies.**

## Question 11

You're reviewing your own code from three months ago — code you largely built by prompting AI and lightly editing the output. You understand maybe 40% of it. Now a bug has appeared in that code and it's yours to fix.

**What's the debugging process here, and what lesson does this moment teach about the long-term cost of Learner A behavior? How does it change how you'll work going forward?**

## Question 12

The exercise says: *"If your only skill is writing code that AI can write, you are already replaceable."*

A fellow student pushes back: *"But AI still needs humans to prompt it, review it, and integrate it — so we're always needed."*

**Who is right? Use the exercise's own reasoning to build your response. What specific skills does the exercise say make you irreplaceable — and does the student's argument account for them?**
