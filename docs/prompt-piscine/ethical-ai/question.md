## Question 1

You've just written your palindrome function from scratch. It works. Now Exercise 1 says to ask AI: *"What's the time complexity? What edge cases might I miss? Are there better approaches?"*

Your teammate skips Step 1 entirely and asks the same question — pasting code they found online.

**You both get the same AI response. But your understanding after reading it is deeper than theirs. Why? What specifically did the struggle in Step 1 give you that made the AI's answer more useful — and what did your teammate receive that they can't fully use?**

## Question 2

You submit your `ex00_reflection.md`. The honest answer to *"Can you explain code you've submitted without AI's help?"* is: sometimes, but not always.

**You're not being graded on that answer — you're being asked to be honest. What does answering this question honestly actually do for you as a learner? What's the risk of being dishonest with yourself here, even if no one else sees it?**

## Question 3

Exercise 2 asks you to extend your palindrome function: ignore spaces and punctuation, make it case-insensitive, return the position where the string breaks. You attempt it. It takes 40 minutes and you get two of the three right.

Then you ask AI: *"Did I miss anything? Can it be more efficient?"*

AI identifies the third case you missed and suggests a cleaner approach.

**Write the reflection the exercise is asking for: what did AI add that you didn't consider? But more importantly — would you have understood AI's suggestion if you hadn't spent those 40 minutes first? Explain why or why not.**

## Question 4

It's the night before a submission deadline. You haven't started `ex04_scenarios.md`. The three scenarios — interview, production bug, new technology — feel abstract to you because you haven't experienced any of them yet.

**The exercise asks you to reflect on real risks. Can you write a genuine reflection on risks you haven't lived through yet? What's the difference between answering these scenarios from imagination versus from actual developed judgment — and which one is the exercise trying to build?**

## Question 5

You're filling out the skills table in Exercise 5. You rate yourself:
- Problem Decomposition: 3/5
- Systems Thinking: 2/5
- Critical Evaluation: 3/5
- Debugging Mindset: 4/5
- Conceptual Understanding: 2/5

The exercise says: choose your **lowest** and write 3 specific actions this week — without AI.

**You have two skills tied at 2/5. How do you decide which one to focus on? And why does the exercise specifically say "without relying on AI" for the improvement plan — what would be lost if you designed your own growth plan by asking AI how to improve?**

## Question 6

Your `README.md` must include: *"One paragraph describing how you plan to apply these principles in future quests."*

Two students write this paragraph. Student A writes about intentions — how they *want* to try problems first, how they *plan* to use AI only after. Student B writes about a concrete system — specific triggers for when they'll open an AI tool, what they'll do for the first 20 minutes of any problem, how they'll test whether they understood.

**Which README paragraph actually fulfills the exercise's purpose — and what's the difference between stating a principle and building a system around it?**

## Question 7

A new library drops. It's two weeks old. AI tools have no reliable training data on it. The documentation is sparse — mostly a GitHub README and two example files.

Exercise 4 asks: *"How do you learn it?"*

**Answer the scenario — not with what you'd hope to do, but with the actual step-by-step process a developer with genuine fundamentals would use. What skills from Exercise 5's table are being directly tested here, and which ones would immediately expose gaps?**

## Question 8

The exercise's final reminder says: *"Learners who use AI to skip learning improve quickly — for a few weeks. Then they plateau."*

You've been coding for three months, mostly using AI to generate solutions and lightly editing them. You're shipping code. Your confidence is growing.

**Are you improving or plateauing — and how would you know the difference from the inside? What specific signal would tell you that your growth is real versus surface-level?**

## Question 10

## Question 11

The exercise distinguishes between two outcomes of using AI:
- Using it to **think deeper**
- Using it to **think faster**

In a professional environment, speed matters. Deadlines are real. Clients don't care how you learned.

**Make the strongest honest argument you can for why "thinking deeper" still matters in a world that rewards speed. Then make the strongest argument for the other side. Which one does the exercise come down on — and do you agree?**
